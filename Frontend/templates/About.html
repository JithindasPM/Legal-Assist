The six fundamental rights

Article 14–18Right to equality

Right to Equality. The Constitution says that the government shall not deny to any person in India equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws. It means that the laws apply in the same manner to all, regardless of a person's status. This is called the rule of law.

Article 19–22Right to freedom

Right To Freedom is covered in Article 19, Article 21A, Article 22 of the Indian Constitution. Each one of those articles Provides Certain Protections and rights. Article 20 – protects against arbitrary arrest and detention. Article 21A – Safeguards the right to life and personal liberty.

Article 23–24Right against exploitation

Article 23 forbids any form of exploitation. Also, one cannot be forced to engage in labour against his/her will even if remuneration is given. Forced labour is forbidden by the Constitution. It is considered forced labour if the less-than-minimum wage is paid.

Article 25–28Right to freedom of religion

Article 25 (Freedom of conscience and free profession, practice, and propagation of religion) Article 25 guarantees the freedom of conscience, the freedom to profess, practice, and propagate religion to all citizens. The above-mentioned freedoms are subject to public order, health, and morality.

Article 29–30Cultural and educational rights

Cultural and educational rights are human rights that promote equality and respect for all cultures, religions, and languages. They include the right to freedom of expression, the right to receive an education, and the right to participate in cultural life.

Article 32–35Right to constitutional remedies

Article 226 along with Article 32 of the Indian Constitution guarantees every citizen the Right to Constitutional Remedies, which includes equality before the law and equal protection of laws. This right is often exercised by filing a Writ Petition in the High Courts and the Supreme Court of India.
